What is CFD?

CFD (a Contract for Difference) is an agreement between a buyer and a seller to exchange the difference between the opening price and closing price of a financial instrument. This instrument allows you to trade this change between the prices of the asset underlying it. Regardless of whether the marker is rising or falling, you can potentially make the profit on price movements.

If you believe that the asset’s price will go up, you open a “buy” position. On the contrary, if in your opinion the asset’s price will decrease, you open a “sell” position. If your forecast was right, you gain a profit.

Binary options and CFDs have much in common. However, there are some differences. The major one is that in binaries you have a predetermined amount of profit or loss. In CFDs you have leverage – so you can get higher profits.

  • High leverage
  • High leverage
  • Ability to hedge your portfolio
  • No day trading requirements
  • Stop Loss/Take Profit option

Account - An account opened by the broker for a client to keep a record of transactions.
Account Balance - The total sum of funds (deposits, withdrawals, and transactions) in client’s trading account.
Ask Price - The price at which a currency pair is offered for sale. It is also known as the “offer”, “ask price”, and “ask rate”.
At the money (At Expiry) - A situation when an option has an execution (strike) price equal or near the current market price of the underlying instrument.
Available Margin - The available amount required to maintain an open position.
Boundary Instruments – Instruments that allow the trader to predict whether or not the price of an asset will fall outside predetermined boundaries when the option expires.
Back Office – The department responsible for the settlement and related processes.
Base Currency – The first currency in the pair. For example, in USD/EUR – the USD is the base currency.
Basic Point – 1% of 1%. It is used to measure interest rates to show the change in the price of an asset.
Bear – A trader who counts on price decline of a particular asset, security or market. The term is often referred to those traders who speculate for a decline in prices.
Bear Market – A market characterized by pessimism, declining prices and widespread selling of underlying assets.
Bearish – A type of traders who believe that a price for a tradable instrument will decline.
Bearish Engulfing – A chart pattern characterized by a bearish candle who’s body exceeds the previous candle’s body.
Bearish Harami – The pattern that consists of a white body and a small black body that is completely inside the range of the white body. It shows that the previous upward trend is coming to an end.
Bearish Reversal – A market situation when a bullish (upward-trending) market begins to move in the opposite direction.
Bid Price – The price at which an underlying instrument can be sold. For example, when trading an EUR/USD currency pair the listing is 1.12545 / 1.25679, the bid price for this currency pair is the first figure of 1.12545, meaning that a seller meaning that a seller will earn 1.12545 dollars for 1 Euro.
Bollinger Bands – A chart created by John Bollinger. It is used in technical analysis to measure high and low prices of the underlying asset regarding historical price.
Bonds – A fixed-income security in which an issuer (usually a company or government ) borrows money, agreeing to pay an interest rate and settle the debt at a specified date. By purchasing bonds investor becomes a creditor of a specific company or government.
Bretton Woods Agreement
of 1944 –		 A landmark system for monetary and exchange rate that established fixed exchange rates for major currencies, pegged the currency rates to the price of gold, and made the U.S. dollar a reserve currency. The Bretton Woods Agreement lasted till 1973.
Broken – A term used to describe a situation when a price breaks a certain critical level.
Broker – An individual or a company that is mediators between buyers and sellers. Brokers charge a fee or commission for their services.
Buba – Bundesbank, Central Bank of Germany
Bull – A trader who counts on price rise of a particular asset, security or market. The term is often referred to those traders who speculate for an increase in prices.
Bull Market – A market characterized by optimism, rising prices and widespread buying of underlying assets.
Bullish – A type of traders who believe that a price for a tradable instrument will rise.
Bullish Engulfing – A chart pattern characterized by a bullish candle which body is less the previous candle’s body.
Bullish Harami – The pattern that consists of a large candlestick followed by a smaller candlestick. It shows that the previous downward trend is coming to an end.
Bullish Reversal - A market situation when a bearish (downward-trending) market begins to move in the opposite direction.
Candlestick – A type of a chart that presents daily high, low, opening and closing prices of a currency pair. Each candlestick is composed of a wide part called “body” and lines above and below the body. The body shows whether opening and closing prices for a given period and the line below shows the lowest price while the line above – the highest price for that timeframe.
Chart - A graphical representation of data, where the data is represented by symbols, such as bars, lines, or slices depending on the chart type.
Capital Markets - A market where buyers and sellers trade medium and long-term financial instruments.
Central Bank – A national bank or entity responsible for financial and banking services, as well as country's monetary policy.
Chart Report - A tool that enables visualization of historical prices.
Chartist – A person who uses charts to forecast future price movements.
Clearing – A procedure that settles an exchange of financial instruments especially between a buyer and a seller.
Closed Position – A completed investment where a buy and sell orders were executed. In result of closed position, a trader receives profit or a loss.
Closing price – The price of a security or a trading pair traded on a given day. This term is also used as a price of the end of a business day.
Closing prices – A plural from “closing price”. It is used to define the price of the last transaction for currency pair or another asset at the end of a trading session.
Commission – An amount of money typically paid to a broker for provided services for negotiating a sale.
Commodities – A plural form of "commodity".
Commodity – A document exchanged by parties of a transaction that defines the terms of this transaction.
Conservative – An investing strategy aimed to preserve the portfolio by investing in low-risk assets or fixed-income defined securities.
Consolidation – A period when prices are less volatile and are moving within a well-defined pattern.
Contract – A standard agreement to buy or sell a certain unit. In trading, it is referred to a unit of trading.
Counterparty – An individual or a bank involved in a transaction of currency or security buying and selling and acts as a guarantor of the contract.
Cross Rate – The exchange rate between two currencies calculated with the use of the third currency. Typically, it is referred to the exchange rate that does not include the U.S. dollar.
Currency – Money issued by a government that represents a tradeable asset.
Currency Depreciation – A significant loss of the currency value.
Currency Pair – Two currencies that can be traded against one another in the forex market.
Currency Risk – A risk of a currency’s price change.
Dealer - An individual or a company who acts as a counterparty in a transaction for buying and selling currencies.
Deficit – A negative balance of trade.
Delivery – A transaction settlement by which a currency must be delivered for the terms of the contract to be fulfilled.
Deposit – A number of funds put on the trading account.
Depreciation – A significant decrease in currency’s value.
Derivative – A contract between two parties with a dependent value from underlying assets.
Devaluation – A significant decrease in the currency's exchange rate due to the market conditions.
ECB - European Central Bank.
Economic Indicator – A piece of economic data used by traders to predict the price movement for a certain asset or currency.
EMU - European Monetary Union.
End Of Day (Mark-to-Market) – The end of the trading day in financial markets.
Euro – A currency adopted by the European Monetary Union (EMU).
Expiry Level – A level after which a contact for an underlying asset is no longer in effect.
Expiry time – The last date and time after which the option can no longer be exercised.
Exposure Coverage – An amount of potential loss covered by funds.
Fed - Federal Reserve.
Fixed Exchange Rate (Representative Rate) – A country’s exchange rate regime where the value of its currency values is officially set to another country's currency, a basket of currencies or to the price of gold. Opposite to the floating exchange rate.
FOMC - Federal Open Market Committee.
Fundamental Analysis – A method of market analysis based primarily on examining external economic, geopolitical, financial situation, social climate, and other factors.
High/Low Instruments – A binary option instrument that allows trader speculate on the price change of the underlying asset either it will be higher or lower the target price at the expiry date.
Investment Amounts – Money invested by a trader in a buy or sell deal.
IMF - International Monetary Fund.
Inflation – A rate that measures a price increase for goods and services.
Initial Margin – The proportion of the total market value of securities that the investor must pay on his own.
Interbank Market – The financial system for trading currencies between different financial institutions.
Interbank Rates – The percentage charged on short-term loans between banks.
Intervention – A situation when a central bank buys or sells a currency in the foreign exchange market to influence its exchange rate.
IRS - Interest Rate Swaps.
Kiwi – the New-Zealand Dollar.
Leading Indicators – Economic indicators that register change and have an impact on the future economic activity. They help traders to forecast price movements. Examples: the Consumer Price Index and official Unemployment Rates.
Libor - London Interbank Offered Rate.
Liquidity – The degree to which an asset can be quickly bought or sold at a given market without changes in its price.
Long (Long Position, to “go long”) – Buying an asset with the anticipation that its price will rise in value.
Market Prices – The current price at the market at which the asset can be bought or sold at a particular time.
Margin – The guarantee required from the trader to maintain his open positions.
Margin Call – A broker's request to the trader to add more funds to maintain his open positions.
Margin Utilization – The portion of the interest rate of the available margin.
Market Maker – A broker who keeps a bid and ask price and is ready for both to buy and the financial instrument at any time.
Market Order – An order for immediate execution at the best current price.
Market Volatility – Degree of variation of trading price changes over time. A higher volatility is associated with a higher risk.
Money Markets – The market where financial instruments with high liquidity are traded by banks and other financial institutions.
Money Supply – Total amount of money in a country's economy in a certain period of time.
MPC - Monetary Policy Committee.
Nickel – The US term for five basis points.
Note – A financial instrument acknowledging a debt that has a longer term than a bill but a shorter term than a bond.
One Touch Instruments – The instrument that allows an investor receive a payout if the price of the underlying asset reaches the target value during the lifetime of the option.
Out of the money
(At Expiry) –		 A term used to describe that the option is not currently profitable. It refers to a call option which strikes price is higher than the market price of the underlying asset, or a put option which strike price is lower. It describes a situation of the incorrect trader’s forecast.
Offer Price – The price at which the underlying asset could be sold.
Open order – An order to buy or sell an underlying asset that has no specific expiration date. Open Position – A position that is still active.
Options – A financial instrument that sets a right, though not an obligation between two parties for the buy (‘Call’) or sell (‘Put’) an asset at a certain price on a specified date.
Order – An instruction from a trader to his broker to buy or sell a certain security.
Points, Pips – The smallest change in a quoted currency price it can make.
Position – An open investment of buying or selling of certain assets for a given price.
Price Manipulation – A situation when the asset’s price is artificially increased or decreased. In most cases, it is illegal.
Profit/Loss (P/L) – The measure of trader’s ability to consistently profit from winning trades. It is calculated as the average profit from winning trades divided by the average loss from losing trades.
Quote – The highest price at which a buyer is willing to pay for a certain asset and the lowest price at which a seller is willing to receive.
Quoted currency – The second currency in a currency pair. For the USD/EUR, EUR is the quoted currency.
Refunds – The amount the investor is refunded within the event that an option expires ‘Out of the Money’.
Return – The gain or loss of a security in a particular period.
Rally – A substantial shift towards the increase in currency value.
Range – The difference (range) between highest and lowest prices of an asset during a certain trading session.
Rate – The price at which a currency can be bought or sold against another currency.
Repo (Repurchase) – The sale of an asset combined with an agreement to repurchase the same asset at a higher price at a future date.
Resistance – A term that refers to a specific price level at which sellers like to try and sell, while buyers are less likely to buy.
Retail Investor – An individual investor who buys and sells small amounts of securities for their personal account rather than for a company.
Risk Management – The process of forecasting, evaluation of financial risks and potential losses as well as procedures to avoid them.
Roll-Over – The situation when the settlement date is moved to the next date.
Risk (Reward ratio) – The ratio between profit potential of a trade relative to its loss potential.
Settlement – The completion of the transaction followed by currency transfer.
Short – Selling a currency with the intention to buy it back when it will decline in value.
Short Position – A trader’s position who’s selling the currency hoping that it will drop in value.
Spot – Buying and selling of currencies at a price for immediate delivery and settlement.
Spot Price – Current price of the currency at which it can be sold right now (in the spot market).
Stop Loss Order – An order to sell a currency when it reaches a certain price level. Stop Loss Order is aimed to limit losses.
Support Levels – Price levels, which are historically lowest in the market.
Swap – The simultaneous buying and selling of the same amount of a currency for two different dates, against the buying and selling of another currency.
Target Prices (Price Target) – The projected level a price of a currency forecasted by advisor or analyst. If the actual price will reach the target price, the investor gets the most of the reward.
Technical Analysis – The method of market analysis that is based on previous market data (like price and volume). It focuses mainly on the internal price factors.
Tick – The difference between the current market price and the last quoted market price at a given time.
Trading plan – A system used by a trader to evaluate markets, define acceptable risks, and setting short and long-term goals.
Trading Station – An application that allows traders quickly and easily access online trading.
Underlying Asset – A financial instrument with a price based on a certain asset.
Underlying Asset Types – Currencies: (e.g. EUR/USD);
Commodities, (e.g. Silver, Crude Oil);
Stocks (e.g. Yahoo!, British Airways);
Indices: (e.g. FTSE 100, NASDAQ);
For full details see the Asset Index.
Unrealized Profit/Loss
(Open P/L) –		 Profit or loss of a position that becomes realized once the position is closed.
US Prime Rate – The interest rate set in the United States by the Federal Reserve Bank. It is the rate that commercial banks charge their most trustworthy clients.
Used Margin – The required amount in your balance reserved to keep your positions open.
Value Date – The date on which the two parties exchange the currencies that were bought or sold. For spot, it is the date of a trade’s settlement.
Volatility – The extent on which the asset price has a tendency to fluctuate.
Volume – The total of trades of a certain instrument or market over a given period.
Wholesale Money – High-volume money borrowed by banks and financial institutions.
Wholesale Price Index – Price changes of a basket of wholesale goods. The index is often used to measure inflation.
Working day – A day (other than Saturday, Sunday and official holidays) on which business can be conducted.
World Bank – A financial institution that provides loans to developing countries.
Writer – The seller of a position.
Yield Curve – The graph describing changes in yield of instruments depending on time to maturity.
